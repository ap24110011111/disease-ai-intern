# Related Work

Machine learning and artificial intelligence have become essential tools in modern healthcare by enabling the early prediction and diagnosis of chronic diseases using clinical and laboratory data. Over the past decade, researchers have explored a wide range of approaches, beginning with traditional machine learning algorithms, progressing to deep learning architectures, and more recently incorporating adaptive and proactive learning techniques. These developments aim not only to improve predictive accuracy but also to provide reliable clinical decision support that can adapt to changing patient conditions. The following review summarizes existing work under three major themes relevant to this research.

## A. Classical Machine Learning Approaches

Traditional machine learning algorithms have been widely used for disease prediction because of their simplicity, interpretability, and effectiveness on structured healthcare datasets. Commonly used algorithms include Logistic Regression, Decision Trees, Random Forests, Support Vector Machines, Gradient Boosting, and ensemble learning techniques. These methods have demonstrated strong performance in predicting diseases such as diabetes, chronic kidney disease (CKD), cardiovascular disorders, and cancer by learning patterns from demographic information, laboratory measurements, and clinical records.

A recent study evaluated eleven supervised learning algorithms for breast cancer recurrence prediction using clinical characteristics and blood test results collected from 342 patients. The models were trained using a training-testing split with three-fold cross-validation to improve reliability. Among the evaluated methods, AdaBoost achieved the highest performance with an AUC of 0.987 and an accuracy of 97.1%. The study further applied SHAP analysis to identify the most influential clinical features affecting recurrence prediction, thereby improving model interpretability. Although the reported performance was excellent, the authors acknowledged limitations such as the relatively small sample size, single-center data collection, and the absence of genetic information, highlighting the need for validation on larger and more diverse datasets.

Similarly, machine learning has been successfully applied to classify patients into clinically meaningful type 2 diabetes subgroups using routinely collected healthcare data. The proposed framework emphasized reproducible model development, careful validation, and robust evaluation procedures to ensure reliable disease stratification. The study demonstrated that well-designed machine learning models can support personalized treatment planning while maintaining consistent prediction performance across different experimental settings. These findings demonstrate that classical machine learning techniques remain valuable because of their interpretability, computational efficiency, and ease of deployment in clinical environments.

## B. Deep Learning Approaches

Deep learning has gained significant attention for disease prediction because neural networks can automatically learn complex nonlinear relationships that are often difficult to capture using conventional machine learning techniques. Multi-Layer Perceptrons (MLPs), Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs), and hybrid deep learning models have been increasingly adopted for analyzing structured clinical data and electronic health records.

One proposed disease prediction framework combined deep learning with ensemble learning methods to improve diagnostic performance. The workflow included data preprocessing, feature extraction, missing-value handling, model selection, and comprehensive evaluation. The deep learning component, based on an MLP architecture, was integrated with powerful ensemble algorithms such as XGBoost and LightGBM. Performance was assessed using accuracy, precision, recall, F1-score, ROC analysis, confusion matrices, cross-validation, and SHAP-based explainability. The integration of neural networks with ensemble learning improved predictive accuracy while preserving model transparency, making the framework more suitable for practical healthcare applications.

These studies demonstrate that deep learning models generally outperform many traditional algorithms when sufficient training data are available. However, they also require greater computational resources, careful hyperparameter tuning, and large datasets to avoid overfitting. Consequently, many recent healthcare studies combine deep learning with explainable AI techniques to ensure that prediction results remain interpretable for clinicians.

## C. Adaptive and Proactive Disease Detection Methods

Adaptive artificial intelligence represents an emerging direction in healthcare by developing prediction systems capable of improving over time as new clinical information becomes available. Unlike conventional static models, adaptive approaches seek to maintain predictive performance in changing clinical environments and support proactive disease management through continuous learning.

Research on chronic kidney disease consistently emphasizes the importance of early diagnosis because CKD often progresses silently until substantial kidney damage has occurred. Machine learning-based prediction systems can identify high-risk individuals at earlier stages, enabling timely intervention and improved treatment planning. Such intelligent diagnostic systems reduce the likelihood of delayed diagnosis and contribute to better long-term patient outcomes.

Another important area of research has investigated the relationship between chronic kidney disease and cardiovascular complications, particularly valvular heart disease. The literature reports that chronic inflammation, vascular calcification, metabolic abnormalities, and declining kidney function contribute to disease progression and significantly increase morbidity and mortality. These findings reinforce the need for intelligent predictive systems capable of adapting to complex clinical conditions while providing reliable decision support for physicians. Adaptive AI methods therefore represent a promising direction for maintaining prediction accuracy as healthcare data evolve over time and for enabling proactive disease detection rather than reactive diagnosis.

## Research Gap

The reviewed studies demonstrate that traditional machine learning, deep learning, and emerging adaptive approaches have significantly improved disease prediction performance. However, several limitations remain. Many existing studies rely on data collected from a single institution, reducing model generalizability across diverse populations. Some models primarily focus on maximizing prediction accuracy without extensive evaluation of robustness and real-world applicability. In addition, adaptive learning strategies capable of continuously improving model performance using new patient data remain relatively underexplored. Therefore, this project focuses on developing and evaluating an optimized Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) model for chronic kidney disease prediction using comprehensive performance evaluation, including accuracy, precision, recall, F1-score, ROC-AUC, confusion matrix analysis, and cross-validation, to provide a reliable framework for proactive disease detection.
